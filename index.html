Bushfires in Australia

This page provides an overview of bushfires that have occurred in Australia during the 2019-20 bushfire season, which has also colloquially been referred to as “Black Summer”, before exploring how climate change may have exacerbated the scale of the bushfires.

Impact of Bushfires on Australian Land Area

This map illustrates the amount of land that was burnt in each state and territory during the season. This has been normalised using the area of each state to provide a percentage of the land burnt. A slider has been provided to allow filtering of states and territories based on the percentage.

Based on the visualisation, the most affected state or territory was the Australian Capital Territory where over a third of the territory's land area was burnt during the bushfire season.

The scale of the damage to the land was substantially more detrimental compared to the other states and territories where the next most affected states were New South Wales and Victoria, which only sustained approximately 6% of damage of the states’ land.

In contrast, Western Australia, South Australia and Tasmania had less than 1% of their land area that was affected by the bushfires in the 2020-21 bushfire season.

The stacked bar chart provides further insight into the distribution of the damage over the months. For New South Wales, Northern Territory and Western Australia, there was an even distribution with a consistent amount of area being burnt over the season.

In contrast, the remaining states and territories either experienced damage throughout a shorter time period as illustrated by Queensland and the Australian Capital Territory, or experienced a strong skew as illustrated by Victoria, where most of the damage was sustained in the first two months.

Together with the map, this chart acts to establish that all of the states and territories were impacted by the fires to differing degrees and at differing timeframes.

Please note: The chart has not been normalised like the map as it aims to illustrate the distribution of the total dmanage sustained by month. Therefore, while it appears that the Northern Territory sustained the most of damage, due to the scale of the territory’s land area, this only amounted to 5% of the land being affected.

Changes in Climate over the years in Australia

Climate change has had a strong impact on many aspects of bushfires such as their frequency and intensity.

For instance, the increased average temperature of the globe, in tandem with decreased rainfall have acted to exacerbate the dry seasons experienced by areas that have historically been susceptible to bushfires.

Below, the key climate factors that have been explored include temperature and rainfall.

Impact of Climate Change on Temperature

The two line charts represents the monthly average maximum and minimum temperature of each state and territory in Australia between the period of 2000-2023.

From 2010 onwards, there have been more instances of extreme heat such as in the case of 2019 and 2022 where there were numerous occurrences of the mean maximum temperatures of a state or territory exceeding 35 degrees celsius. A similar trend can be observed in min temperature.

In terms of how the temperature may have affected the Black Summer, Australian Capital Territory had a consistent average maximum temperature of 31.6 degrees for 3 consecutive months between the period of December 2019 and February 2020. This extended period of extreme heat coincided with the peak damage sustained by the territory during the bushfire season, where over

30% of its area was affected by the bushfires as visible in the map above. This is bolstered by the fact that most of the damage was sustained in February 2020. This effectively highlights how the sustained extreme temperatures were a key factor that contributed to the devastating bushfire season.

Impact of Climate Change on Rainfall

The heatmap below depicts the mean monthly rainfall experienced by each individual state and territory, as well as nationally between 2000-2023.

As seen in the heatmap, with the exception of February 2022 which was an outlier, overall the average rainfall has decreased in most states, notably in the last 5 years.

In terms of how the rainfall may have affected the Black Summer, in December 2019, Australia only had an average of 21.69mm of rainfall despite having 64.21mm in 2018 and 99.31mm in 2017.

The reduced rainfall in 2019, resulted in the notably dry season which would have contributed to the circumstances leading up to Black Summer as it would have allowed the fires to spark and travel more easily.

Based on the insights gained above, it was evident that the devastating reach of 2019-20 bushfires was not by coincidence, but rather was due to an accumulation of changes to Australia’s weather and environment as a result of climate change.
